Three nested cycles, two drawn rings. The club keeps going round — the repetition is the mechanism, so a line would have misstated the product. The issue goes out and comes back: shared with people you know (7:1 outward, but always to the town, my mum, the 49 other women — never a public), and the one you made last is what the next one starts from. The verb is share, not hand out: “hand out” decodes as a physical copy, and the corpus’s own circulation verb is share — a quilting group literally posts “comment ACCESS and we will message the link for our issue,” and guild publications are e-mailed. Link carries the browsable-online fact; share keeps the warmth and the known-recipient register. That is the paper’s fourth recommendation, curate exemplar objects, in plain speech; it is deliberately not “it brings in the next member,” which 72 documents could not attest once. Three issues, and you host a club — the inner ring, because what piles up in the middle while the outer one turns is issues. The clock is the only asset here the hero doesn’t carry — the four ways the study found people actually access a craft — and its rim words were running off the face at both widths.

Ship three issues, and you earn a club of your own — and you host it.

The shelf was decoration under a subhead; it is now the block. Its two captions say legitimate peripheral participation in plain English, and they narrate growth the only way this tribe does — backwards, and as a position in a sequence, never as “you will become.” Circle and Editor are now gone from the page entirely: editor misdescribes the job (nobody edits anyone else’s work) and it is one of the school words the red team flagged against “Nobody lectures.” host replaces it on corpus evidence — 37 uses against 1 for leader, with a live verb form (“my personal rules for hosting knit night”) and the structural reason that the host is a kind of member, never a kind of role. It is used only as a verb attached to the club, because hosted on its own correlates with zero-obligation drop-in. The pull-quote is now Tergel’s, the same sentence the maker board carries — a first-person account of an exemplar object doing its work.
A collapsed stack becomes a full spread, and the block is scoped honestly: every one of these photographs is from one night in San Francisco. The heading is the issue’s own reflection question — nobody was asked for a testimonial, so these are answers, not marketing. This is the block that carries the company’s reason for existing without a word of it appearing.
